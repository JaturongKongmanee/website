Jaturong Kongmanee

I design and develop an interactive machine-learning-aided data labeling framework with Professor Mark H. Chignell (lucky to be supervised by him). Taking into account individual differences (e.g., working memory) in judging instances selected by machine learning model, this framework is to develop a psychological space of spatial and geometric representations of raters, to characterize the structures and attributes of raters’ different aspects of labeling expertise. We develop our work based on insights, concepts, and techniques from Psychometrics and Mathematical Psychology, Psychophysics, Behavioural Economics, Statistics, and Machine Learning. (My formal job title is Ph.D. candidate at the University of Toronto.)

I spent a memorable year prior to my Ph.D. at UofT conducting research on Deep Learning, Multi-Objective Optimization, and Evolutionary Algorithm (EA), under the supervision of Professor Kalyanmoy Deb and Professor Vishnu Naresh Boddeti at the Computer Science and Engineering Department at Michigan State University.

I received my master's degree in computer science under the supervision of Professor Rattikorn Hewett from the Red Raiders land, Texas Tech University. I published one ASE paper presenting a general technique for utilizing model checking (an automatic technique for verifying finite-state reactive systems), to identify possible security breaches of a smart contract in a financial blockchain system.

Research


My research goal is to enhance elements of active learning (also known as optimal experimental design): semi-supervised training procedure for calibrated models; adaptive sampling for highly unlabanced class distribution; unsupervised learning of latent feature space; and development of a rater space. Anomaly detection of evolving data exfiltration tactics in the domain of cybersecurity, is a practical problem of my interest to attack because of its characteristics: labels are hard to come by (especially for edge cases); unlabanced class distribution; model calibration is essential; data and concept drift; and the use of scarce domain expertise needs to be as efficient as possible.

Below is the list of representative papers contributing to these elements.

  • A Human-AI Interaction Dashboard for Detecting Potentially Malicious Emails
  • Unsupervised Early Sampling Helps Focus Human Expertise in Active Learning of Anomalies
  • A Simplified and More Efficient Semi-Supervised Training Procedure for Calibrated OOD Detection
