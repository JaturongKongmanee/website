Research


My research goal as a scholar is to work on a problem that sufficiently matters, one that makes a real point. Current research problem of my interest is to develop methods for estimating plausible cognitive brain aging trajectories based on cognitive game-based assessment scores, and for predicting expert judgements when labeling complicated cases.

A prior research problem of my interest was to enhance elements of active machine learning, also known as optimal experimental design, for detecting and predicting anomalies and rare events. This approach was ideal due to inherent challenges of these problems: class distribution is highly unbalanced; model calibration is essential; data and concept drifts; expert labels are scarce (especially for edge cases)--thus the use of expert judgements must be as efficient as possible.

Below is the list of representative papers contributing to these elements of active learning.
  • A Human-AI Interaction Dashboard for Detecting Potentially Malicious Emails
  • Unsupervised Early Sampling Helps Focus Human Expertise in Active Learning of Anomalies
  • A Simplified and More Efficient Semi-Supervised Training Procedure for Calibrated OOD Detection
